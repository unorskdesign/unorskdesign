Why are you working?

If you’ve been in software development for some time, you have most likely already been on the other side of a job interviewing process. Sitting at the other side of the table in front of a sweating and stuttering job candidate. If your company is small and doesn’t have an HR department, it just you plus one or two persons that are taking a decision if the candidate is a right choice.

After the interview is done and we are comparing our notes, more than on several occasions I heard a feedback like this: “[S]he’s what we need …and by the way, [s]he’s making games/creating compilers/etc after work”

And honestly, I couldn’t care less. As long as a company I am working at doesn’t create games or compilers.

The thing is that the two most important things that candidates should be scored for are: why they work and technical skills they have. It’s motivation and abilities. While checking bubble-sort skills and knowledge of popular frameworks is straightforward, I don’t think I am exceptionally good at finding out if a candidate’s motivation levels are. Most of my technical colleagues don’t possess this skill either.

Being able to do something and willing to do it and doing it well - that’s an ultimate combo. And if you’re making games after work, well, you’re a nerd, but this is not important. If you are technically solid that’s already enough for me.

But as I mentioned earlier - you are sometime put in a position where you have to play a huge part in the final decision. When it happens you need to remember that motivation is as important as technical knowledge. A good motivation is when work at your company has a positive personal outcome for you. It's all about finding out what a candidate cares about and if your company has it.
